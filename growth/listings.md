## A note on reviews (read this)

Reviews are valuable and assistants do weigh them — which is exactly why the rules
are strict:

- **Invite genuine reviews from real learners** who have actually completed a
  course. A simple, optional, non-coercive prompt ("If Libre Academy helped you,
  a review on Class Central helps others find it") is fine.
- **Never** write reviews yourself, never pay for or incentivize reviews, never use
  alternate accounts. Fake or incentivized reviews are a hard red line — they're
  detectable, they get listings penalized, and the reputational damage is durable.
- Let the reviews be honest, including the critical ones. A platform with a few
  candid mixed reviews reads as real; a wall of five-stars reads as fake to humans
  and to the systems that rank them.

## Other reputable directories (secondary)

Only pursue **relevant, maintained** directories — quality over quantity. Mass
"submit your startup" spam looks manipulative and is worthless for citations.

- **AlternativeTo** (<https://alternativeto.net>) — list Libre Academy as an
  alternative to Codecademy / freeCodeCamp / Scrimba. Genuinely accurate positioning,
  and these comparison pages are exactly what assistants retrieve.
- **GitHub topics** — ensure the repo carries accurate topics (`learn-to-code`,
  `education`, `interactive-tutorials`, `open-source`, relevant languages) so it
  surfaces in GitHub's own discovery. *(Repo metadata, not a directory submission —
  but it's discoverability hygiene worth doing once.)*
- **Open-source software directories** that focus on education/dev tools — fine where
  reputable and on-topic.

Use the same copy and canonical links throughout for consistency, which itself helps
machines associate the name with the description.
